# Fernlight — Marketing Agency Plan

*A working playbook for what we do, who we serve, how we land clients, and how we grow. Written so anyone joining the team can get the full picture in one read.*

> **The one-line version:** We find hot leads for local businesses — people who've already raised their hand and are ready to talk about what they need. Everything else we build is just the machinery that delivers those people to your door.

---

## 1. What Fernlight Is

**Fernlight brings local businesses hot leads — people who've already expressed interest and are ready to talk about their needs.** That's the product. That's what the client is actually buying: a steady flow of interested prospects landing in their inbox and on their calendar, instead of chasing strangers who never asked to hear from them.

We're not in the business of handing clients more chores. They don't want a website project or an ad account to babysit — they want *customers*. So we own the whole machine that produces them and hand back the only thing that matters: qualified people who want to buy.

**How we actually produce those leads** (the means, not the message):

1. **A site built to convert** — modern, fast, and engineered so the interested visitor takes the next step instead of bouncing. For clients with a weak site or none at all, this is the part of the machine that turns attention into a captured lead.
2. **Ads that put the offer in front of the right people** — paid campaigns (Meta and Google) that drive interested prospects to that site.
3. **Instant, automated follow-up** — every new lead gets contacted in under a minute, so interest converts into a real conversation before it cools.

The combination is the whole point. A website with no traffic captures nobody. Ads pointing at a page that doesn't convert burn money. Leads with no fast follow-up go cold. We run all three together so the client gets one thing out the other end: **hot leads, ready to talk.**

**Who it's for:** Local businesses in the San Diego area that are clearly underinvested online (outdated or missing websites, little to no paid advertising) but operate in **higher-ticket verticals** where one new customer is worth a lot. We're starting with **the trades** — plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs, carpenters, handymen, and similar contractors. One new job is worth hundreds to thousands (a water-heater install, a panel upgrade, an AC replacement), these owners live and die by their next call, and most have a weak online presence — so the math on paying for leads is easy for them to say yes to.

**The expansion path:** Win San Diego trades first → widen to adjacent local verticals (other home services, then real estate, med spas, legal, etc.) → then expand geographically beyond SD.

## 2. Who We Serve (and Why We Niche Down First)

**Ideal first client:**
- San Diego–area trades business — plumber, electrician, HVAC, carpenter, handyman, or similar contractor (owner-operator or small crew)
- Weak or no website, little or no paid advertising
- Actively wants more jobs (a working pro, not a hobbyist)
- Has the volume and margin to happily pay per qualified lead (and later, a cut of closed deals)

**Why we start narrow instead of "any business":**
- **One message, one funnel, repeatable.** We're not reinventing the pitch every call.
- **Referrals compound.** Trades pros know other trades pros. A tight vertical turns one happy client into a pipeline.
- **Case studies transfer.** A win for one contractor directly sells the next one.
- **We get fast.** Doing the same kind of build and the same kind of ad campaign repeatedly makes delivery quicker and cleaner.

Spreading across ten industries at once means ten times the messaging, ten funnels, and no compounding. We go deep before we go wide.

## 3. What We Sell (Offer & Pricing)

We sell one outcome — **a steady flow of hot leads** — and we roll it out in **two phases**. Phase 1 (now) keeps it dead simple and risk-free for the client: they pay a low fee per qualified lead. Phase 2 (~a month out) graduates willing clients to a commission model where they pay nothing per lead and we earn a cut of the deals those leads close.

### Phase 1 — Pay-per-lead *(launch now)*
The whole pitch is "**you only pay for qualified leads.**" We build the lead-capture page, run *and fund* the ads, and the client is charged a flat fee for each qualified lead we hand them. No retainer, no ad budget for them to manage — the only money they put up front is a small setup fee.

- **Small setup fee — ~$250–$750 one-time.** Covers building the lead-capture page and wiring up GoHighLevel (instant text-back, missed-call recovery). Signals commitment and covers our build time.
- **Flat fee per qualified lead.** Priced low relative to the value of a job — a single lead costs the client a fraction of what the job it could land is worth (hundreds to thousands for a trade).
- **We fund the ad spend.** That's what makes "only pay for results" real for the client — but it means our per-lead fee has to comfortably clear our own cost per lead from the ads (see the margin note in Section 6).
- **Define "qualified" up front** to avoid disputes: a real, reachable prospect in the service area who asked for a service we cover (e.g., submitted the form / requested a quote). Spam, wrong area, and out-of-scope requests don't count and aren't billed.

### Phase 2 — Commission on closed deals *(~30+ days out, opt-in)*
Once a client has seen the leads convert and trusts us, we offer an alternative: **stop paying per lead — pay a percentage of each deal that closes instead.** Bigger upside for us when their close rate and ticket are strong, and it aligns us completely with their actual revenue. We roll this out to a **subset of willing clients**, not everyone.

- **No upfront per-lead charge.** We generate the leads, the client works them, and we take an agreed **% of each closed deal**.
- **Runs on GoHighLevel.** Every lead lands in the client's GHL sub-account and moves through a pipeline (New → Contacted → Quoted → **Closed Won**, with a deal-value field). That pipeline is our **source of truth for what closed** and what it was worth.
- **Needs a contract + attribution discipline:** define what counts as a "close," the attribution window, the client's obligation to keep the pipeline current, and our audit rights. Trades have no public-record verification (unlike, say, real-estate deeds), so CRM control + trust + a clear agreement are how we verify — which is exactly why Phase 2 is for a trusted client, not a cold one.

### Why phased
- **Phase 1 removes all buyer risk and gets us proof fast** — simple to sell, simple to deliver, and it builds the case studies that make every next sale easier.
- **Phase 2 captures more value** where the trust and the numbers justify it, without forcing a commission deal on clients who'd rather keep it simple.

### Pricing the first clients
Offer a **founder rate** on Phase 1 (a reduced or waived setup fee, or a discounted per-lead price) to land the first one or two clients and earn case studies — but keep it **paid**. A paying client, even at a discount, takes the work seriously and proves real demand. A free client proves nothing and trains you to give the work away. Raise prices the moment you have one result to point to.

## 4. How We Get Clients (The Outreach Engine)

This is our **client-acquisition system** — completely separate from what we deliver. It's deliberately cheap so we can bootstrap with almost no money.

### The list
- ~1,000 San Diego–area businesses scraped into a CSV: `business_name`, `phone`, `email`, `website`, `industry` — sorted by industry, prioritizing weak/no web presence.
- We work **one segment at a time**, starting with the trades.

### Primary channel: conversational SMS (via GoHighLevel)
- We send from a **local 619 number** — it reads as a real San Diego local, which gets far higher reply rates than a toll-free line. (An 800 number screams "marketing blast.")
- The approach is **conversational, not a broadcast.** The first text only has to earn a reply; we take it from there to a booked call.
- **Requires A2P 10DLC registration before any texts send** (see Section 7). Real gate — budget a few days for approval.

### The SMS sequence

**1. Opener** *(the only message that fires automatically)*
> Hey, is this {{business_name}}?
*(append the opt-out line — e.g. "...reply STOP to opt out")*

Short on purpose — short texts get the highest reply rate, and "is this you?" almost always pulls a "yeah, who's this?"

**2. After they reply — intro + qualify**
> Yeah hey, I'm Gio — based here in SD. Quick one: are you running your own marketing/lead gen, or do you have someone on it?

**3. The pivot — the pitch**
> Gotcha. So I help San Diego trades businesses pull in more inbound jobs — sharper site + automated follow-up so the right calls come to you instead of you chasing them. Took a look at your setup and there's a couple easy wins. Want me to send 'em over, or easier to hop on a quick 10-min call?

**4. Book the call**
> Perfect — here's my calendar, grab whatever slot works: {{link}}

**No-reply bump** *(after 24h, once only)*
> Hey {{business_name}}, still the right number? Not a bot, promise — just had a quick idea for you.

**Clean exit on a no** *(protects the number)*
> All good, appreciate the reply. Around if it's ever useful — Gio

**How it runs in GHL:** Only message 1 goes out from the campaign. Replies land in the Conversations inbox and we handle 2–4 live (saved as snippets for speed). The no-reply bump can be an automated wait-24h → send step.

### Complementary channels *(my suggestions)*
- **Cold email** — we already have emails in the list. No A2P gate, easy compliance (identify sender, real opt-out), and it runs in parallel to multiply reach.
- **Referrals** — build a referral ask into delivery from day one. In a tight vertical like the trades, this becomes the cheapest and best channel fast.
- **Local presence** — we're SD-based; in-person drop-ins and local networking punch above their weight for high-ticket local clients.

## 5. How We Deliver (Fulfillment)

Everything here is the machinery behind the promise — the client sees leads; we run the parts that produce them.

- **Websites:** Standardize on one fast builder so the first builds are quick and repeatable *(tool TBD — Framer / Webflow / WordPress; pick one and template it).*
- **Ads:** Build campaigns in Meta/Google Ads Manager, point them at the new site or a dedicated lead funnel, optimize weekly.
- **Follow-up automation:** Each client gets their **own GHL sub-account** — the same platform we prospect with becomes the delivery vehicle (lead capture, instant text-back, nurture). One stack runs both sides of the business.
- **Reporting:** A simple weekly snapshot per client — qualified leads delivered, and (for Phase 2 clients) deals worked and closed. Proves the value in black and white and makes the Phase 2 commission conversation easy.

### What a client is worth *(illustrative)*

**Phase 1 — pay-per-lead:**
- Setup: **~$250–$750** one-time
- A flat fee per qualified lead (lock the number once we know our real ad cost per lead — it has to sit comfortably above it)
- A client taking, say, 20–40 qualified leads a month is recurring revenue with little ongoing labor once the campaign is running

**Phase 2 — commission:**
- **% of each closed deal**, no per-lead charge
- Upside scales with the client's ticket and close rate — one strong trades client closing several jobs a month off our leads can be worth far more than the per-lead fee would have been

### The margin reality *(because we now fund the ads)*
In Phase 1 we pay for the ad spend, so the model only works when **price-per-lead > our cost-per-lead.** The first job after launch is to find each campaign's real cost per qualified lead and set the client fee above it with margin to spare. Until that gap is proven on a live campaign, treat early ad spend as **paid R&D** — start small and scale a campaign only once its per-lead math works.

### The bootstrap logic
Client acquisition stays cheap (SMS). The shift from the old retainer idea is that **in Phase 1 we front the ad spend** instead of the client — so this is no longer zero-cash. Plan for a modest ad-spend float to get campaigns to the point where per-lead revenue covers per-lead cost; the setup fees help offset it. Once a campaign's math is proven, per-lead revenue funds the ongoing ad spend, and Phase 2's commission stacks higher-margin revenue on top. Keep early budgets small and let proven unit economics — not optimism — decide when to scale spend.

## 7. Unit Economics & The Margin Risk *(Phase 1)*

Phase 1 only works if **what we charge per qualified lead beats what the ads cost us to produce one.** Because we front the ad spend, that gap *is* the business. This section makes the math concrete and names the risk so we manage it on purpose — not by surprise.

### How the per-lead math actually works
Two costs sit under every qualified lead:
1. **Raw cost per lead** — what Meta/Google charge to generate one form fill or call.
2. **Qualification drag** — not every raw lead is billable (junk, out of area, out of scope). If only 2 of every 3 raw leads qualify, our true cost per *qualified* lead is the raw cost ÷ 0.67.

So: **our cost per qualified lead = raw CPL ÷ qualification rate.** The client fee has to clear *that* number with margin to spare.

### Illustrative scenarios *(estimates to validate on a live test — not gospel)*

| Trade | Typical job value | Est. raw CPL | Qual. rate | Our cost / qualified lead | Fee we charge | Margin / lead | Fee as % of job |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Handyman | $150–$400 | ~$12 | 70% | ~$17 | $30 | ~$13 | ~10% of a $300 job |
| Plumber | $400–$1,500 | ~$30 | 65% | ~$46 | $75 | ~$29 | ~9% of an $800 job |
| HVAC repair | $300–$900 | ~$35 | 65% | ~$54 | $80 | ~$26 | ~13% of a $600 job |
| Electrician | $300–$2,000 | ~$40 | 60% | ~$67 | $100 | ~$33 | ~10% of a $1,000 job |
| HVAC / panel install | $5,000–$12,000 | ~$70 | 60% | ~$117 | $200 | ~$83 | ~3% of an $8,000 job |

Read the last two columns together: even a $200 lead fee is a rounding error against an $8k install — which is exactly *why the client happily says yes*. And the higher the ticket, the more margin room we have and the better a Phase 2 commission deal looks later.

### The risk, stated plainly
This is the central risk of the whole Phase 1 model: **we spend real money on ads before we know our true cost per qualified lead, and we only get paid after we deliver billable leads.** Several things can squeeze or invert the margin:

- **CPL comes in higher than estimated.** The table is a guess until a live campaign proves it. A competitive San Diego trade in peak season can run 2–3× these numbers.
- **Qualification rate is worse than hoped.** Pay for 100 leads, only 45 billable → cost per qualified lead nearly doubles.
- **Disputes.** If the client argues a lead wasn't "qualified," we ate the ad cost and can't bill it — which is why the written definition in Section 3 matters.
- **Cash-flow timing.** We pay the ad platforms now and invoice leads later; a fast-scaling client can mean real money out the door before any comes back.
- **Concentration.** One campaign running underwater can quietly eat the margin from the ones that work.

**Worked example of the squeeze:** we price a plumber's lead at **$75**, expecting a ~$46 cost. If real raw CPL lands at **$55** and only **55%** qualify, our true cost is **~$100 per qualified lead** — every lead we deliver now *loses ~$25*, and the harder we scale, the more we lose. Nothing in the campaign "looks" broken; the math just quietly went negative.

### How we keep it safe
- **Test before scale.** Every new client/trade starts on a **small fixed test budget** (e.g., $300–$500). Compute the *actual* cost per qualified lead before spending another dollar.
- **Price off a margin multiple, not a hope.** Set the fee at **≥ ~1.7–2× our proven cost per qualified lead**, so normal CPL drift doesn't wipe out the margin.
- **Price per trade, not one flat fee.** A handyman lead and an HVAC-install lead have wildly different economics — don't average them.
- **Set a CPL ceiling per campaign** and pause/rework anything that crosses it instead of letting it bleed.
- **Cap the float.** Decide up front the most we'll front to an unproven campaign (ties to the open decision in Section 12).
- **Tighten qualification criteria in writing** so billable-lead disputes are rare.
- **Graduate the winners to Phase 2.** High-ticket, high-close clients are worth more on commission than per-lead — moving them captures upside *and* removes our ad-spend risk on that account once they trust us.

## 8. Compliance & Risk *(read this — no surprises)*

Being straight so we go in with eyes open.

- **A2P 10DLC is mandatory** to send business SMS to US numbers. Brand + campaign registration, ~3–7 day approval, and it requires real legal info (an EIN, or sole-proprietor registration under a legal name + US address + a phone OTP). **No texts deliver until it clears.**
- **Consent is the real issue.** Cold outreach to a scraped list means recipients never opted in. That creates **TCPA exposure** (statutory damages per text) and means the carrier consent question can't be honestly answered "yes." This is **legally grey territory** — we operate it informed, not blind.
- **Number health.** Spam complaints degrade a number fast; carriers suspend senders whose opt-out rate crosses ~3%. The conversational opener and clean exits keep complaints low, but practically, **cold-outreach numbers are semi-disposable** — expect to rotate them, and **never run the core business identity through the blasting number.**
- **Guardrails we always keep:** identify ourselves, include an opt-out, exit gracefully on a no, and never use the prospecting number for anything we can't afford to lose.
- **The clean long-term path:** as we grow, the lowest-risk acquisition is inbound + referrals + opt-in lead magnets, which sidestep all of the above. **SMS is the scrappy bootstrap, not the forever plan.**

## 10. 30-Day Launch Plan — to First Paying Client

Tight and milestone-driven. The goal of month one is **one paying client** — not a big build-out.

**Week 1 — Setup**
- Pull the trades segment out of the 1,000-lead list
- Buy the 619 number ✅ *(done)* → submit A2P 10DLC registration
- Finalize the SMS sequence, build the GHL campaign + booking calendar
- Build one strong sample/portfolio site to show on calls

**Week 2 — Launch outreach**
- A2P approved → start texting the first ~200–300 trades leads
- Work replies live, book discovery calls
- Track: reply rate, call-book rate

**Week 3 — Close**
- Run the discovery calls, present the **Phase 1 pay-per-lead offer**
- Goal: **close 1 founder-rate client**
- Collect the setup fee, kick off their lead-capture page

**Week 4 — Deliver + prove**
- Ship the page, launch and **fund** their ads, deliver the first qualified leads
- Dial in cost-per-lead vs. our fee → confirm the margin works
- Capture early results → **first case study**, and use it to sharpen the pitch for client #2

**Success for month 1:** one paying Phase 1 client live, leads flowing, the per-lead margin proven on a real campaign, and one case study in progress. That's validation. Everything scales from a proven, repeatable win — so we **resist adding verticals, channels, or Phase 2 until this loop works once.**
